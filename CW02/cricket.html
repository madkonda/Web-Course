Cricket & consistency

Cricket is one of my favorite hobbies because it rewards steady improvement. Small upgrades to footwork, timing, and fitness add up over a season — the same way small upgrades in fundamentals add up in software engineering.

Jump to: Practice | Gear | Watching | Innovative shots | Media

Practice basics

When I practice, I keep it simple: warm up, focus on one skill, then finish with a short match simulation. It helps me stay consistent without overcomplicating the plan.

  • Warm-up: light jogging + mobility
  • Batting focus: timing and footwork drills
  • Fielding: quick pickups and throws
  • Fitness: short sprints for endurance
  • Notes: one thing to improve next time

Gear checklist (ordered list)

Before a session, I like a simple checklist so I don’t forget anything.

  1. Bat and gloves
  2. Stumps (if we’re setting up)
  3. Ball(s)
  4. Water bottle
  5. Warm-up band / tape

Watching & learning

I enjoy watching cricket to study decision-making: when to attack, when to defend, and how players manage pressure. Even highlights can teach timing and shot selection.

“Stay balanced, watch the ball, and trust the practice.”

I apply the same idea in software: keep fundamentals strong, then move fast with confidence.

Innovative cricket shots

Modern cricket has evolved with creative batting. Here are a few fun examples (with simple local images), inspired by this article: “10 Innovative Cricket Shots That Evolved During Recent Times” .

Quick list: Paddle scoop, Dilscoop, Periscope, Switch hit, Helicopter, Reverse scoop, Upper cut, Inside out, Short arm jab, Paddle sweep

1) Paddle scoop

A creative way to score fine runs by guiding the ball into open space.

2) Dilscoop

A modern scoop variation that forces field changes and opens other scoring areas.

3) Periscope

A creative option during powerplay when gaps are small and timing matters.

4) Switch hit

A stance switch to target new gaps and disrupt a bowler’s plan.

5) Helicopter shot

A powerful wristy follow-through that helps convert yorkers into scoring shots.

6) Reverse scoop

A high-skill shot that uses pace and angles to score where fielders aren’t.

7) Upper cut

A controlled response to short balls: angle the bat to send it behind square.

8) Inside out

Create room with footwork and hit through the line into the open field.

9) Short arm jab

A short, powerful jab that can find boundaries without a huge swing.

10) Paddle sweep

A delicate shot that uses the bowler’s pace/spin to guide runs behind the square.
